Careful with the monster

Don’t be evil was google’s motto, until they became… less than good. In my ignorant opinion, don’t be evil is a bad motto, not because people should be evil, but because bad stuff happens way before we get evil. Instead, we should try not to serve Moloch.

Moloch, game theory, ‘the road to hell is paved with good intentions’, are all ways of pointing out the same problem (at least when applied to social interactions). But of course the rhetorical effect of them is vastly different. In this difference we find why don’t be evil simply does not suffice.

When people hear about the famous prisoner’s dilemma, they understand that cooperating is worse for an individual but better for the group. I think this framing might miss the point.

When people hear ‘the road to hell is paved with good intentions’, they understand that even when u try to do good you might bring harm. I think this framing might miss the point.

Moloch instead represents a monster. A monster that is fed whenever people do anything that is not a socially optimal strategy.

The point that is missed by classic the first two formulations, is that these negative effects are always there. Even if some action does not seem to fit in the framing of the prisoner’s dilemma, or even carry an intention. I think this is where the Moloch framing rhetorically out-performs the other forms of the same message.

This monster represents the negative effects of these actions. These effects are might also be responsible for things we ‘feel’ but cannot quite put our fingers on, such as the value of human work being degraded, all being traded for efficiency. These effects are also much more beautifully and articulately described in Allen Ginsberg’s poem howl.

As by definition feeding this monster goes against doing what is best for the collective, I pose feeding this monster should be avoided in general. Unfortunately, feeding this monster is often necessary for individual’s to thrive. As such, we should be careful when feeding this monster.
